Cell biology is the branch of biology that studies the structure, function, and behavior of cells — the fundamental units of all living organisms. Every known form of life is composed of one or more cells, from the simplest single-celled bacteria to the estimated 37 trillion cells that make up the human body. Understanding the cell is therefore central to understanding life itself: how organisms grow and develop, how they respond to their environment, how they reproduce, and how diseases arise and can be treated.

The field has been transformed over the past century by advances in microscopy — from the light microscopes that first revealed the cellular organization of tissues in the seventeenth century, to electron microscopes that exposed the ultrastructure of organelles in the mid-twentieth century, to the fluorescence microscopy and live-cell imaging techniques of today that allow scientists to watch molecular processes unfold in real time inside living cells. Combined with molecular biology and biochemistry, modern cell biology has achieved a detailed understanding of the machinery of life at the molecular level.

Cell Theory and History

The cell was first observed by Robert Hooke in 1665, when he used a primitive compound microscope to examine thin slices of cork and noticed the tiny box-like compartments he named "cells" (from the Latin cella, meaning small room). Antonie van Leeuwenhoek, using superior single-lens microscopes of his own construction, subsequently observed single-celled organisms — "animalcules" — in pond water and other materials, providing the first glimpse of the microbial world. However, the recognition that cells are the fundamental unit of all life came only in the mid-nineteenth century, through the work of botanist Matthias Schleiden, zoologist Theodor Schwann, and physician Rudolf Virchow.

Schleiden (1838) proposed that all plants are composed of cells; Schwann (1839) extended this to animals, arguing that cells are the universal structural unit of life. Virchow's contribution (1855), often expressed as omnis cellula e cellula ("every cell from a cell"), established that new cells arise only from the division of pre-existing cells — ruling out spontaneous generation and linking all living organisms through chains of cell division extending back to the earliest life. This trio of ideas constitutes the cell theory, one of the foundational principles of biology. The subsequent development of cell staining techniques, improved microscopes, and ultimately electron microscopy progressively revealed the internal complexity of cells and laid the groundwork for modern cell biology and molecular medicine.

Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic Cells

All cells fall into one of two fundamental categories: prokaryotic and eukaryotic. Prokaryotic cells — found in bacteria and archaea — are structurally simpler, typically 1–10 micrometers in diameter, and lack a membrane-bound nucleus; their genetic material (a single circular DNA molecule) is found in a region of the cytoplasm called the nucleoid. Prokaryotes also lack most of the membrane-bound organelles characteristic of eukaryotic cells, though they do possess ribosomes (smaller than those of eukaryotes, with a sedimentation coefficient of 70S) and, in many species, a complex cell wall. Despite their structural simplicity, prokaryotes display extraordinary metabolic diversity and occupy virtually every habitat on Earth.

Eukaryotic cells are generally larger (10–100 micrometers) and far more structurally complex than prokaryotes. Their defining feature is a membrane-bound nucleus that houses the cell's DNA (organized into multiple linear chromosomes) and separates transcription (in the nucleus) from translation (in the cytoplasm). Eukaryotic cells also contain a rich array of membrane-bound organelles, each with specialized functions. The endosymbiotic theory, developed by Lynn Margulis, proposes that several key eukaryotic organelles — particularly mitochondria and chloroplasts — were once free-living prokaryotes that were engulfed by ancestral eukaryotic cells and gradually became permanent, indispensable components. Strong evidence for this theory includes the fact that mitochondria and chloroplasts possess their own circular DNA, their own ribosomes (of the prokaryotic 70S type), and reproduce by binary fission.

Cell Organelles and Their Functions

Eukaryotic cells are populated by a variety of organelles, each a distinct compartment with specialized biochemical functions. The nucleus, bounded by a double membrane perforated by nuclear pores, contains the cell's genomic DNA organized into chromatin and is the site of DNA replication and transcription. The nucleolus within the nucleus is a dense region where ribosomal RNA is synthesized and ribosome assembly begins. Mitochondria, often called the powerhouses of the cell, are the sites of cellular respiration — the oxidative breakdown of glucose and other fuels to generate ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the cell's principal energy currency. Mitochondria have a double membrane; the inner membrane is highly folded into cristae and contains the proteins of the electron transport chain and ATP synthase.

The endoplasmic reticulum (ER) is a network of membranous tubules and cisternae that ramifies throughout the cytoplasm. The rough ER, studded with ribosomes, is the site of synthesis of membrane proteins and secretory proteins; the smooth ER is involved in lipid synthesis, detoxification, and calcium storage. The Golgi apparatus receives proteins from the ER, further modifies them (by glycosylation, phosphorylation, and other post-translational modifications), sorts them, and dispatches them to their final destinations — the plasma membrane, lysosomes, or secretory vesicles. Lysosomes are membrane-bound bags of hydrolytic enzymes that digest macromolecules brought in by endocytosis and break down damaged organelles in a process called autophagy. The cytoskeleton — a dynamic network of protein filaments including actin microfilaments, intermediate filaments, and microtubules — gives the cell its shape, enables cell movement, facilitates intracellular transport, and provides the machinery for cell division.

Cell Division: Mitosis and Meiosis

Cell division is essential for growth, tissue repair, and reproduction. Eukaryotic cells divide by one of two processes: mitosis or meiosis. Mitosis is the form of cell division used for growth and repair, producing two daughter cells that are genetically identical to the parent cell and to each other. The cell cycle consists of interphase (during which the cell grows and duplicates its DNA) followed by mitosis (nuclear division) and cytokinesis (cytoplasmic division). Mitosis itself is conventionally divided into prophase, prometaphase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase, in which the duplicated chromosomes (each consisting of two sister chromatids joined at the centromere) are progressively condensed, aligned at the cell equator, separated, and distributed to the two daughter nuclei.

Meiosis is the specialized form of cell division that produces gametes (sperm and eggs) and is confined to sexually reproducing organisms. Unlike mitosis, meiosis involves two successive rounds of division — meiosis I and meiosis II — following a single round of DNA replication. The key event of meiosis I is the separation of homologous chromosome pairs (rather than sister chromatids), which halves the chromosome number from diploid (2n) to haploid (n). During prophase I, homologous chromosomes pair up and undergo crossing over — the exchange of segments between non-sister chromatids — which shuffles genetic information and is a major source of genetic variation. The random alignment of homologous pairs at metaphase I (independent assortment) provides additional variation. The result of meiosis is four haploid cells, each genetically unique, that mature into gametes. Fertilization restores the diploid chromosome number and generates a genetically novel individual.

Cell Signaling and Communication

Cells do not operate in isolation: they constantly send and receive chemical signals that coordinate their activities within tissues and organs and allow the organism as a whole to respond to its environment. Cell signaling typically involves three stages: reception, transduction, and response. A signaling molecule (ligand) — such as a hormone, neurotransmitter, or growth factor — binds to a specific receptor protein, either on the cell surface (for hydrophilic signals that cannot cross the plasma membrane) or inside the cell (for hydrophobic signals such as steroid hormones that diffuse through the membrane). Binding of the ligand changes the receptor's conformation and activates intracellular signaling pathways.

Intracellular signal transduction typically involves cascades of molecular switches — often protein kinases that phosphorylate and thereby activate or inactivate downstream proteins — amplifying the initial signal and integrating it with other signals the cell is receiving. Second messengers such as cyclic AMP (cAMP), inositol triphosphate (IP₃), and calcium ions (Ca²⁺) relay signals from the cell surface to the nucleus and other intracellular targets. The ultimate cellular response may be a change in gene expression, a metabolic adjustment, cell movement, secretion, proliferation, or programmed cell death (apoptosis). Failures in cell signaling underlie many diseases: cancer, for example, frequently arises from mutations that cause growth factor receptors or downstream signaling proteins to be constitutively active, driving uncontrolled cell proliferation.
