Logic

Logic

Logic is the branch of philosophy concerned with the principles of valid inference and correct reasoning. It asks not merely whether a conclusion is true, but whether it follows from the premises offered in support of it — whether the argument's structure compels assent regardless of the subject matter involved. In this sense, logic is the grammar of reason itself: the discipline that makes explicit the rules by which sound thinking operates.

The Western logical tradition begins with Aristotle, whose Organon established the systematic study of the syllogism. From Aristotle it passed through the Stoics, who developed propositional logic, through the medieval logicians who made Aristotelian logic the cornerstone of university education, to Leibniz's dream of a universal calculus of reasoning, and finally to the modern mathematical logic of Frege and Russell, which underlies all of computer science and formal mathematics.

The Aristotelian Syllogism

Aristotle defined logic as the science of demonstration — of showing why something must be the case given what we already know. His central instrument was the syllogism: a form of argument in which a conclusion follows necessarily from two premises that share a common term.

The most famous syllogism in philosophy illustrates the structure:

RoleProposition
Major premiseAll men are mortal.
Minor premiseSocrates is a man.
ConclusionTherefore, Socrates is mortal.

The power of the syllogism lies in its form: if both premises are true and the form is valid, the conclusion cannot be false. No inspection of the particular content is required — the structure alone guarantees the inference. Aristotle catalogued the valid syllogistic forms (called moods) organized by figures according to the position of the common (middle) term.

The Four Categorical Propositions

Aristotelian logic operates on four types of categorical proposition, traditionally designated by the vowels A, E, I, O:

TypeFormExample
A — Universal affirmativeAll S are PAll humans are mortal
E — Universal negativeNo S are PNo fish are mammals
I — Particular affirmativeSome S are PSome philosophers are wise
O — Particular negativeSome S are not PSome rulers are not just

The relationships among these four forms — what follows from what, what contradicts what — are displayed in the Square of Opposition, one of the most elegant diagrams in the history of philosophy.

Validity and Soundness

One of logic's most important distinctions is between validity and soundness:

  • An argument is valid if the conclusion follows necessarily from the premises — that is, if it is impossible for the premises to be true and the conclusion false. Validity is a structural property; it does not require the premises to actually be true.
  • An argument is sound if it is both valid and its premises are in fact true. A sound argument guarantees a true conclusion.
Valid but Unsound

The following argument is valid (the conclusion follows from the premises) but unsound (the first premise is false): "All cats are dogs. Socrates is a cat. Therefore, Socrates is a dog." Much philosophical confusion arises from failing to distinguish these two properties.

Propositional Logic

While Aristotelian logic analyzes the internal structure of propositions (subject, predicate, quantifier), propositional logic — developed by the Stoics and later formalized in the nineteenth century — treats whole propositions as the basic units and studies the logical connectives that combine them.

Logical Connectives

The five principal connectives of propositional logic are:

ConnectiveSymbolRead asTrue when
Negation¬Pnot PP is false
ConjunctionP ∧ QP and Qboth P and Q are true
DisjunctionP ∨ QP or Qat least one of P, Q is true
ConditionalP → Qif P then QP is false, or Q is true
BiconditionalP ↔ QP if and only if QP and Q have the same truth value

A tautology is a propositional formula that is true under every possible assignment of truth values to its variables — for example, P ∨ ¬P (the law of excluded middle) and ¬(P ∧ ¬P) (the law of non-contradiction). These laws, foundational since Aristotle, are among the most basic commitments of classical logic.

The Law of Non-Contradiction

Aristotle considered the law of non-contradiction — that a thing cannot both be and not be in the same respect at the same time — the most certain of all principles, the one that underlies all rational discourse. He argued that it cannot be strictly proven (since any proof must use it), but can be defended dialectically by showing that anyone who denies it cannot coherently say anything at all.

Aristotle, Metaphysics IV

"It is impossible for the same thing to belong and not belong to the same thing at the same time and in the same respect." This is the firmest of all principles — the one from which all demonstration ultimately depends.

Predicate Logic

Predicate logic (or first-order logic), developed rigorously by Gottlob Frege in his Begriffsschrift (1879), extends propositional logic by analyzing the internal structure of propositions. It introduces quantifiers — symbols that range over individuals in a domain — enabling the full expression of Aristotelian syllogistic and far more besides.

The two fundamental quantifiers are:

  • Universal quantifier (∀): "For all x, ..." — written ∀x P(x), meaning every object in the domain satisfies predicate P.
  • Existential quantifier (∃): "There exists an x such that ..." — written ∃x P(x), meaning at least one object in the domain satisfies predicate P.

The classical syllogism "All men are mortal; Socrates is a man; therefore Socrates is mortal" becomes, in predicate logic:

Syllogism in Predicate Logic

∀x (Man(x) → Mortal(x))
Man(Socrates)
∴ Mortal(Socrates)

Frege's formalization made possible the logicist program of Bertrand Russell and Alfred North Whitehead — the attempt, in Principia Mathematica (1910–1913), to derive all of mathematics from purely logical axioms.

Informal Fallacies

Alongside the formal study of valid argument, the logical tradition has always catalogued fallacies — patterns of reasoning that appear to be valid but are not. Medieval logicians organized these extensively; their relevance to rhetoric, law, and theology made the study of fallacies a practical discipline.

Ad hominem: Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself. Ad populum: Appealing to popular opinion or majority belief as a substitute for argument. Straw man: Misrepresenting an opponent's position in order to refute a weaker version of it. Appeal to authority: Treating the testimony of an authority as decisive in a domain outside their competence.

Begging the question (petitio principii): Assuming in one's premises the very conclusion one is trying to prove. False dilemma: Presenting only two options when more exist. Slippery slope: Claiming that one step will inevitably lead to a chain of increasingly dire consequences without establishing the causal links. Hasty generalization: Drawing a universal conclusion from an insufficient sample.

Equivocation: Using the same word in two different senses within a single argument. Amphiboly: Exploiting grammatical ambiguity in a premise. Composition: Inferring that a whole has a property because its parts have it. Division: Inferring that the parts have a property because the whole has it.

Logic in the Western Tradition

The study of logic has occupied a central place in Western education from antiquity through the present. For the ancient Greeks, logic was the tool by which philosophy justified its claim to be a science. For the medieval university, it was the first of the seven liberal arts — the prerequisite to all other learning. For the early modern natural philosophers, it was the method by which reliable knowledge could be distinguished from mere opinion. And for modern mathematics and computer science, formal logic is the very foundation of proof and computation.

Logic and the Liberal Arts Tradition (click to expand)

The medieval university organized its lower division — the trivium — around three arts: grammar, rhetoric, and logic (dialectic). Grammar taught how to speak correctly; rhetoric, how to speak persuasively; logic, how to speak truly — how to construct arguments that compel assent because they are valid. Logic was the queen of the trivium precisely because truth is the end of all serious discourse. The recovery of Aristotle's complete logical writings in the twelfth century (the logica nova) transformed European intellectual life and contributed directly to the development of scholastic philosophy and the university system.
