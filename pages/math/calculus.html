Calculus

Calculus is the mathematical study of continuous change. Developed independently in the late seventeenth century by Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz — one of the great intellectual achievements in the history of Western civilization — calculus furnishes the language in which the laws of nature are written. Its two central operations, differentiation and integration, are related by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, one of the most profound results in all of mathematics.

Newton developed his "method of fluxions" to solve problems in mechanics and gravitation. Leibniz approached the same problems through a more geometric lens and introduced the notation $dy/dx$ and $\int$, which remain standard today. The dispute over priority between the two men and their respective national camps was bitter and long-lasting, but the ideas themselves proved so fertile that mathematics has never been the same.

Limits

The concept of a limit is the rigorous foundation on which all of calculus rests. The limit of a function $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $a$ is the value that $f(x)$ approaches as $x$ gets arbitrarily close to $a$ (but is not required to equal $a$):

$$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$$

The formal ($\varepsilon$-$\delta$) definition: For every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a $\delta > 0$ such that $0 < |x - a| < \delta$ implies $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$.

Key Limit Laws

If $\lim_{x\to a} f(x)$ and $\lim_{x\to a} g(x)$ both exist, then: the limit of a sum is the sum of the limits, the limit of a product is the product of the limits, and the limit of a quotient is the quotient of the limits (provided the denominator's limit is nonzero).

Important Limits

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1 \qquad \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{e^x - 1}{x} = 1 \qquad \lim_{x \to \infty}\left(1 + \frac{1}{x}\right)^x = e$$

Differential Calculus

The derivative of a function $f$ at a point $x$ is defined as the limit of the difference quotient:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$

Geometrically, the derivative at a point is the slope of the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at that point. Physically, if $f(t)$ represents position, then $f'(t)$ is instantaneous velocity and $f''(t)$ is acceleration.

Applications of Derivatives

The derivative is the principal tool for optimization — finding maxima and minima. At a local extremum, $f'(x) = 0$ (or $f'$ is undefined); these are called critical points. The second derivative test: if $f'(c) = 0$ and $f''(c) > 0$, then $f(c)$ is a local minimum; if $f''(c) < 0$, it is a local maximum.

Integral Calculus

The definite integral of $f$ from $a$ to $b$ is defined as the limit of Riemann sums:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*)\,\Delta x$$

Integration Techniques

Substitution (u-substitution)

When the integrand contains a composite function, let $u = g(x)$, $du = g'(x)\,dx$:

$$\int f(g(x))\,g'(x)\,dx = \int f(u)\,du$$

Example: $\int 2x\cos(x^2)\,dx = \int \cos(u)\,du = \sin(u) + C = \sin(x^2) + C$

Integration by Parts

The integral analog of the product rule:

$$\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$$

Choose $u$ and $dv$ using the LIATE priority: Logarithms, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential.

Example: $\int x e^x\,dx = xe^x - \int e^x\,dx = xe^x - e^x + C = (x-1)e^x + C$

Partial Fractions

To integrate a rational function $P(x)/Q(x)$, decompose it into simpler fractions whose integrals are known. For example:

$$\frac{1}{x^2-1} = \frac{1}{(x-1)(x+1)} = \frac{1/2}{x-1} - \frac{1/2}{x+1}$$ $$\int \frac{1}{x^2-1}\,dx = \frac{1}{2}\ln|x-1| - \frac{1}{2}\ln|x+1| + C = \frac{1}{2}\ln\!\left|\frac{x-1}{x+1}\right| + C$$

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus reveals that differentiation and integration are inverse operations — the two great ideas of calculus, which appeared to be separate, are in fact two faces of a single coin. It has two parts:

Part I: If $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$, then $F'(x) = f(x)$ wherever $f$ is continuous. That is, differentiation undoes integration.

Part II: If $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$ (meaning $F' = f$), then:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

This result — that the definite integral can be computed by evaluating an antiderivative at the endpoints — transforms an otherwise intractable limiting process into a practical calculation. It is one of the most powerful computational tools in mathematics.

Common Integrals

$$\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \neq -1)$$ $$\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C$$ $$\int e^x\,dx = e^x + C$$ $$\int \sin x\,dx = -\cos x + C$$ $$\int \cos x\,dx = \sin x + C$$ $$\int \frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx = \arctan x + C$$

Improper Integrals

When the interval of integration is infinite, or when the integrand has a singularity, we use improper integrals, defined as limits:

$$\int_1^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^2}\,dx = \lim_{b\to\infty} \int_1^b \frac{1}{x^2}\,dx = \lim_{b\to\infty}\left[-\frac{1}{x}\right]_1^b = 0 - (-1) = 1$$

An improper integral either converges (the limit is finite) or diverges. The integral $\int_1^\infty 1/x\,dx$ is a famous example of a divergent improper integral, connected to the harmonic series.
