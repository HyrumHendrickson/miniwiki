Quantum mechanics is a fundamental theory in physics that describes the physical properties of nature at the scale of atoms and subatomic particles. It is the foundation of all quantum physics, including quantum chemistry, quantum field theory, quantum technology, and quantum information science.

At very small scales, quantum mechanics differs significantly from classical physics. It introduces concepts such as wave-particle duality, quantization of energy, and the uncertainty principle, which have no analog in classical mechanics.

Historical Development

Quantum mechanics emerged over the first three decades of the twentieth century through the work of many physicists confronting anomalies that classical physics could not explain.

Core Principles

Wave-Particle Duality

All quantum objects exhibit properties of both waves and particles. The de Broglie wavelength of a particle with momentum $p$ is:

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$$

where $h$ is Planck's constant ($h \approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34}$ J¬∑s).

The Schr√∂dinger Equation

The time-dependent Schr√∂dinger equation governs the evolution of a quantum state $\psi$:

$$i\hbar \frac{\partial \psi(\mathbf{r},t)}{\partial t} = \hat{H}\,\psi(\mathbf{r},t)$$

where $\hat{H}$ is the Hamiltonian operator. For a particle in a potential $V(\mathbf{r})$:

$$\hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(\mathbf{r})$$

The time-independent Schr√∂dinger equation for stationary states is:

$$\hat{H}\,\psi = E\,\psi$$

The wave function $\psi$ must satisfy $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |\psi|^2\,d^3r = 1$, since $|\psi(\mathbf{r},t)|^2$ gives the probability density of finding the particle at position $\mathbf{r}$.

Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

Conjugate observables such as position and momentum cannot both be measured to arbitrary precision simultaneously:

$$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}$$

Similarly, for energy and time:

$$\Delta E \cdot \Delta t \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}$$

This is not a statement about measurement precision, but an intrinsic feature of quantum systems.

Superposition and Entanglement

A quantum system can exist in a superposition of states. If $|\psi_1\rangle$ and $|\psi_2\rangle$ are valid states, so is $\alpha|\psi_1\rangle + \beta|\psi_2\rangle$ for any complex $\alpha, \beta$ with $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$.

Two particles can be entangled such that the quantum state of each cannot be described independently. A maximally entangled Bell state is:

$$|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\bigl(|00\rangle + |11\rangle\bigr)$$

Particle in a Box

One of the simplest exactly solvable quantum systems is the infinite square well (particle in a box). For a particle confined to $0 \leq x \leq L$, the energy levels are quantized:

$$E_n = \frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2mL^2}, \quad n = 1,2,3,\ldots$$

The corresponding normalized wave functions are:

$$\psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)$$

Interpretations

The Copenhagen interpretation (Bohr, Heisenberg) holds that the wave function is a complete description, and physical properties do not have definite values prior to measurement. Upon measurement, the wave function "collapses" to an eigenstate.

The many-worlds interpretation (Everett, 1957) posits that all possible outcomes of quantum measurements are realized in branching, non-communicating worlds. No wave function collapse occurs.

The pilot wave (de Broglie‚ÄìBohm) theory is a deterministic hidden-variable theory. Particles have definite positions guided by a pilot wave satisfying the Schr√∂dinger equation.
