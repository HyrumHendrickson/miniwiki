A musical instrument is any device constructed or adapted to make musical sounds. The history of musical instruments is as old as human culture itself: the earliest archaeological evidence of musical instruments — bone flutes carved from bird or bear bones discovered in caves in Germany and Slovenia — dates to approximately 43,000 years ago, contemporaneous with the earliest known figurative art, suggesting that music-making was part of the behavioral repertoire of anatomically modern humans from very early in our history. Musical instruments have been created in extraordinary diversity across human cultures, reflecting local available materials, acoustic environments, and aesthetic traditions.

The standard academic classification system for musical instruments is the Hornbostel-Sachs system, developed by Erich Moritz von Hornbostel and Curt Sachs and published in 1914. It classifies instruments into five major categories based on the physical mechanism by which sound is produced: chordophones (vibrating strings), aerophones (vibrating air columns or airstreams), membranophones (vibrating membranes), idiophones (the instrument body itself vibrates), and electrophones (sound produced or amplified electronically). This classification system, refined and extended over the decades, provides a consistent framework for comparing instruments across all the world's musical traditions.

String Instruments (Chordophones)

Chordophones produce sound through the vibration of one or more stretched strings. They are classified by how the strings are set in motion: in bowed chordophones (viols, violins, cellos, double bass), a horsehair bow drawn across the string creates a sustained tone through stick-slip friction; in plucked chordophones (lutes, guitars, harps, sitars, koto, balalaika), the string is set in motion by finger or plectrum; in struck chordophones (dulcimers, cimbalom, piano), the string is struck by a hammer or mallet. The violin family — violin, viola, cello, and double bass — forms the backbone of the Western symphony orchestra. The violin, in its modern form stabilized around the seventeenth century by luthiers of the Cremona school (Amati, Stradivari, Guarneri), has been regarded as perhaps the most expressive of all instruments, capable of an extraordinary range of tone colors and dynamic nuances.

The piano — or pianoforte (Italian for "soft-loud," reflecting its ability to vary dynamics, unlike the earlier harpsichord) — was developed around 1700 by Bartolomeo Cristofori, who replaced the harpsichord's quill-plucking mechanism with a hammer action that caused string volume to depend on the velocity of the key stroke. The piano became the dominant keyboard instrument of the Classical and Romantic eras and the central instrument of private music-making and musical education, an unequaled vehicle for solo composition and performance. Plucked chordophones of extraordinary variety appear in virtually every musical culture: the lute was the most prestigious Renaissance instrument, the guitar its natural successor in the modern era; the sitar and sarod dominate North Indian classical music; the oud pervades the Middle East and North Africa; and the guqin and pipa have central roles in Chinese musical tradition.

Wind Instruments (Aerophones)

Aerophones produce sound through the vibration of a column of air. They are divided by the mechanism that sets the air in motion. In flutes and flute-type instruments (transverse flutes, recorders, ocarinas, panpipes), the player directs a stream of air across an edge or opening, causing periodic oscillations. In reed instruments, a vibrating reed (or pair of reeds) interrupts the airflow and sets the air column in resonance: single-reed instruments (clarinet, saxophone) use a single reed vibrating against a flat mouthpiece; double-reed instruments (oboe, bassoon, cor anglais, bagpipe chanters) use two reeds vibrating against each other. In brass instruments (trumpet, French horn, trombone, tuba), the player's lips act as the vibrating element, buzzing against a cup- or funnel-shaped mouthpiece; pitch is controlled by adjusting lip tension and by valves or a slide that change the tube length.

The woodwind section of the modern orchestra typically includes flutes, oboes, clarinets, and bassoons (each in pairs or more), while the brass section includes horns, trumpets, trombones, and tuba. Beyond the orchestra, wind instruments appear in military and marching bands, jazz ensembles, and virtually every folk tradition. The clarinet, invented around 1700 by Johann Christoph Denner, became a favorite solo instrument of Mozart (who wrote a celebrated concerto and quintet for it) and is central to jazz and klezmer music. The saxophone, invented by Adolphe Sax in the 1840s and patented in 1846, became an iconic voice of jazz and popular music. Non-Western aerophones of particular note include the bansuri (bamboo flute of India), the shakuhachi (Japanese end-blown bamboo flute), the didgeridoo (Australian Aboriginal drone instrument), and the Chinese erhu (though that is technically a chordophone) and dizi flute.

Percussion Instruments (Membranophones and Idiophones)

Percussion instruments are struck, shaken, scraped, or rubbed to produce sound, and they encompass both membranophones and idiophones. Membranophones produce sound through the vibration of a stretched membrane: drums in endless variety are the archetypal membranophones, ranging from the orchestral timpani (kettledrums), whose pitch can be adjusted by changing membrane tension, to the snare drum, bass drum, and tom-toms of the modern drum kit, to the tabla and mridangam of Indian classical music, the djembe of West Africa, and the taiko of Japan. Idiophones produce sound through the vibration of the instrument body itself: they include xylophones and marimbas (wooden bars of graduated size struck with mallets), metallophones (metal bars, as in the vibraphone and glockenspiel), bells, gongs, cymbals, castanets, triangles, and clappers.

Percussion instruments are among the oldest in human history, and their cultural significance extends far beyond their musical function. Drums have served in warfare, religious ritual, long-distance communication, and communal celebration across the world. The rhythmic complexity achievable by master drummers in West African, Afro-Cuban, Brazilian, and North Indian traditions far exceeds what most Western listeners would initially appreciate: polyrhythmic textures involving simultaneous cross-rhythms in different meters, precise interlocking patterns, and improvisatory dialogue between musicians represent sophisticated artistic achievements requiring years of training. The integration of African rhythmic traditions into jazz, blues, rock, and popular music through the African diaspora has been one of the most consequential processes in the history of modern music.

Keyboard Instruments

Keyboard instruments use a keyboard — a row of levers (keys) — to control sound production, which may involve strings (harpsichord, clavichord, piano), pipes (organ), or electronics (synthesizer, electronic organ). The pipe organ is one of the oldest and most imposing of all keyboard instruments: by pumping air through pipes of varied length and material, it can produce sounds of great power and variety, from a whisper to a roar. The largest organs, found in cathedrals and concert halls, have thousands of pipes and multiple keyboards (manuals) plus a pedalboard. The organ was the dominant keyboard instrument of the Renaissance and Baroque, and Bach's organ works — toccatas, fantasias, fugues, and chorale preludes — are among the supreme achievements of keyboard music.

The harpsichord, in which strings are plucked by quills, was the standard keyboard instrument of the Baroque era and served as the continuo instrument in ensemble music — providing the harmonic foundation over a figured bass line. It was superseded by the fortepiano (early piano) in the Classical period; modern interest in historically informed performance has brought about a revival of harpsichord performance. The clavichord, a small and intimate keyboard instrument in which strings are struck by tangents, was prized for its expressive dynamic control and was used primarily as a practice and private performance instrument. The accordion, with its free reeds set in motion by a bellows, bridges the categories of keyboard and aerophone and is central to the folk music of many cultures from Argentine tango to French musette to Tejano conjunto.

Electronic and Electronic-Acoustic Instruments

Electronic instruments generate or modify sound through electronic means. The theremin, invented by Russian physicist Léon Theremin in 1920, was one of the first electronic instruments: it is played without physical contact, the performer moving their hands in the electromagnetic field surrounding two antennas to control pitch and volume. The ondes Martenot (1928) similarly produced eerie, expressive electronic tones and was used by composers including Messiaen. The Hammond organ (1935), using spinning electromagnetic tone wheels, became a staple of jazz, gospel, and rock music. The synthesizer — an instrument that generates audio signals that may be shaped into almost any sound — was first made practical by Robert Moog in the 1960s; the Moog synthesizer and its successors transformed popular music and film scoring. Digital synthesizers (notably the Yamaha DX7, 1983) and samplers (which play back recorded sounds triggered by a keyboard) further democratized sound production.

The rise of digital audio workstations (DAWs) — software platforms that allow the recording, editing, processing, and mixing of audio on a standard computer — has transformed music production since the 1990s, enabling solo producers to create sophisticated multi-track recordings without traditional studio infrastructure. MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface), a standard communication protocol established in 1983, allows electronic instruments and computers to exchange performance data, enabling the integration of keyboards, drum machines, synthesizers, and computers into flexible hybrid systems. Electric versions of traditional acoustic instruments — the electric guitar (pioneered by Les Paul, the Gibson ES-150, and Fender Telecaster and Stratocaster), electric bass, and electric violin — transformed popular music in the twentieth century, enabling the amplified ensembles of rock and roll, jazz fusion, and country music.
