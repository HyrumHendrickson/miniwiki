Classical Virtue Ethics

Virtue Ethics
FieldEthics, Moral Philosophy
OriginAncient Greece
Key figuresSocrates, Plato, Aristotle, Aquinas

GoalEudaimonia (flourishing)

Virtue ethics is the oldest and, many would argue, the most profound tradition of moral philosophy in the Western world. Unlike modern ethical theories that focus on the rightness of actions (deontology) or the maximization of outcomes (consequentialism), virtue ethics centers on the character of the moral agent: what kind of person should one be? What habits of soul, cultivated over a lifetime, constitute human excellence?

Rooted in Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics and refined through the Stoic and Christian traditions, virtue ethics holds that the moral life is not primarily about following rules or calculating consequences, but about becoming the sort of person who consistently and reliably does what is right, noble, and good — and who takes genuine pleasure in doing so.

Eudaimonia: Human Flourishing

The central concept of Aristotelian ethics is eudaimonia, commonly translated as "happiness" but better rendered as flourishing or human excellence in activity. For Aristotle, every human action aims at some good; the question is what good is ultimate, pursued for its own sake and not as a means to anything further. His answer is eudaimonia.

Crucially, eudaimonia is not a subjective feeling but an objective condition — a manner of living and acting that constitutes the full actualization of distinctively human capacities. A man who spends his life in pleasurable but degrading pursuits is not flourishing, whatever he may feel. A man who cultivates wisdom, exercises justice, practices courage, and maintains friendships of mutual virtue is flourishing, even if his circumstances are difficult. This is not an elitist claim but a dignified one: every human being has the capacity for the kind of excellence that constitutes the good life.

The Four Cardinal Virtues

The classical tradition — consolidated by Plato, developed by Aristotle, and integrated into Christian moral theology by Aquinas — identifies four cardinal virtues (from the Latin cardo, "hinge"), on which all other virtues depend:

Prudence (phronesis, practical wisdom) is the master virtue — the intellectual capacity to discern the right course of action in particular circumstances. It is not merely theoretical knowledge but the cultivated ability to perceive what a situation calls for and to act accordingly. Without prudence, the other virtues go astray: courage without prudence becomes recklessness; justice without prudence becomes rigidity. Prudence is the virtue that governs all the others.

Justice (dikaiosyne) is the consistent disposition to give each person what is due to them — in Ulpian's famous Roman formulation, to render to each his own (suum cuique tribuere). For Aristotle, justice is both a particular virtue (governing dealings with others) and a kind of general virtue (the whole of virtue in relation to others). A just society is one in which citizens are ordered toward the common good and each receives what is proportionate to his contribution and need.

Fortitude (courage, andreia) is the disposition to face danger, hardship, and death with appropriate resolve — neither with reckless bravado nor with cowardice. Aristotle famously defined courage as the mean between rashness (excess) and cowardice (deficiency). The courageous man fears what he ought to fear, to the degree he ought, and acts in spite of it when the cause is good. Fortitude is also the virtue that sustains all the other virtues in the face of difficulty.

Temperance (sophrosyne) is the ordering of the appetites — particularly pleasure and desire — in accordance with reason. The temperate man takes appropriate pleasure in food, drink, and bodily enjoyment, neither ascetically rejecting legitimate pleasures nor enslaving himself to them. Temperance is the virtue of integration: the person whose desires are well-ordered is free; the person whose desires are disordered is a slave to them, however wealthy or powerful he may appear.

The Doctrine of the Mean

Aristotle's most distinctive contribution to virtue theory is the doctrine of the mean (meson): virtue lies between two corresponding vices, one of excess and one of deficiency. The virtuous person habitually hits the appropriate middle point, which is determined not by arithmetic calculation but by practical wisdom in the particular situation.

Virtue as Habit

The most practically important teaching of virtue ethics is that virtues are not inborn but acquired through habituation. Aristotle's famous statement: "We become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, courageous by doing courageous acts." The moral life is not primarily a matter of having the right beliefs or following the right rules, but of training the character through repeated action until virtuous conduct becomes second nature.

This has profound implications for education, culture, and politics. A society that consistently rewards vice and penalizes virtue will produce vicious citizens. A family that models and practices the virtues will, over time, form virtuous children. A culture that celebrates the goods of the soul over the goods of fortune will produce human beings capable of genuine flourishing. The formation of character is not incidental to civilization but central to it.

The Theological Virtues

Christian moral theology, particularly as systematized by Aquinas, accepted the four cardinal virtues wholesale from the classical tradition and added three theological virtues — faith, hope, and charity (love) — infused by God's grace and directed toward the supernatural end of eternal life with God. The theological virtues do not replace the cardinal virtues but perfect and elevate them, orienting the entire moral life toward its ultimate goal.

Saint Paul's enumeration in 1 Corinthians 13 — "And now abideth faith, hope, charity, these three; but the greatest of these is charity" — provides the scriptural foundation. Charity (caritas, love of God and neighbor) is for Aquinas the form of all the virtues: it gives the other virtues their ultimate orientation and meaning. An act of courage performed for the love of God and neighbor is a fully virtuous act in the Christian sense; an act of courage performed for personal glory alone falls short.

The Contemporary Revival

After centuries of eclipse by Kantian deontology and utilitarian consequentialism, virtue ethics underwent a major revival in the latter half of the twentieth century. Alasdair MacIntyre's After Virtue (1981) argued that modern moral philosophy had failed precisely because it had severed moral language from the classical tradition of virtue and human nature that gave it its meaning. Without a conception of human nature and its proper end, moral discourse degenerates into mere expression of preference and the interminable conflicts of the culture wars.

MacIntyre's diagnosis resonates widely because it is correct. The renewal of the virtue tradition — taking seriously the classical and Christian inheritance on human excellence — is not nostalgic conservatism but intellectual and moral realism. The questions Aristotle asked are still the right questions: What is the good for man? What habits of soul constitute that good? What communities and institutions make the cultivation of those habits possible?
