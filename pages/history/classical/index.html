Classical History

Classical History
ScopeAncient Greece and Rome, c. 800 BC – AD 476
PerspectiveThe classical world as the formative root of Western civilization
Key inheritancePhilosophy, democracy, republicanism, rhetoric, law

The ancient Greeks and Romans are not merely historical curiosities. They are the intellectual and political ancestors of Western civilization, and a serious education in the Western tradition begins with them. Athens gave the world philosophy, drama, democracy, and the scientific impulse; Rome gave it law, republican government, engineering, and the administrative framework that carried Christian civilization across the known world. Together they constitute the classical inheritance that every generation of the West has returned to in its greatest periods of cultural achievement — the Renaissance, the Reformation, the American founding.

Articles here treat the ancient world as a living source of wisdom and political insight, not merely a subject of antiquarian interest. The questions Thucydides asked about power and justice, the constitutional problems Cicero grappled with, the moral philosophy Aristotle systematized — these remain the right questions and the right starting points for serious thought.
